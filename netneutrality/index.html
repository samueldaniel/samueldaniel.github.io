"A Series Of Tubes"

Samuel Daniel
samuel.daniel@tufts.edu
COMP-150IDS - Fall 2013 - Tufts University

"And again, the Internet is not something you just dump something on. It's not a truck. It's a series of tubes."

~ Senator Ted Stevens
Senate Commerce Committee Hearing
28 June 2006 [1]

On June 28, 2006, a hearing in the Senate Commerce Committee had reached a stalemate. The committee had voted 11 to 11 on an amendment to a telecommuncations bill that would have prevented Internet service providers from charging extra fees to companies wanting better access to the Internet. Senator Ted Stevens, a member of the committee, had voted against the amendment, arguing that "The regulatory approach is wrong. Your approach is regulatory in the sense that it says 'No one can charge anyone for massively invading this world of the Internet'."[1] Stevens' believed that Internet service providers (the owners and operators of the aforementioned "tubes") should be able to directly manage packets coming from high-traffic sources, and even charge these customers more for Internet access. The senator offered some anecdotal evidence to support his position: "I just the other day got, an Internet was sent by my staff at 10 o'clock in the morning on Friday and I just got it yesterday. Why? Because it got tangled up with all these things going on the Internet commercially."[1]

(An explanation of why the Senator's Internet took so long to reach him is beyond the scope of this paper. Click here for details.)

Senator Steves' oversimplified metaphor for the inner workings of the Internet immediately became a rallying cry for those opposed to his views. The Daily Kos, a progressive-leaning blog about American politics called the Senator an "ignoramus".[2] Other detractors labeled Stevens as "old and out-of-touch".[3] The "web zine" Boing Boing labeled Stevens a "neutricidal maniac" in reference to Stevens' clumsily stated opposition to the idea known as net neutrality. [4]

Network Neutrality: Defined and Debated

Net neutrality is a network design principle. Tim Wu, a professor at Columbia Law School, presents an articulate definition of net neutrality on his website:

"The idea is that a maximally useful public information network aspires to treat all content, sites, and platforms equally. This allows the network to carry every form of information and support every kind of application. The principle suggests that information networks are often more valuable when they are less specialized - when they are a platform for multiple uses, present and future."[5]

The debate that inspired Senator Stevens and his opponents focuses on whether or not the principle of net neutrality should be enforced by laws and regulations governing Internet service providers. Advocates of net neutrality believe that Internet service providers should not be able to control or restrict how their customers use the Internet. Such advocates feel that net neutrality needs to be legally enforced in order to ensure a competitive marketplace and to protect the future growth and development of the Internet. Opponents of net neutrality argue that existing laws and regulations are more than capable of handling any issues of anti-competition. They also believe that further regulation of the Internet would actually negatively affect its future.[6]

A loss of net neutrality for the Internet and the World Wide Web would mean that Internet service providers would be able to prioritize certain types of Internet traffic over others, potentially penalizing websites and services that did not pay extra fees for their extra bandwidth usage. ISPs would be able to provide higher quality service to the prioritized data, but consumers wanting to send and receive non-prioritized data would be met with much slower speeds and a degraded quality of service.

Despite Senator Stevens' apparent ignorance, some reporters and scholars came to the defense of his "tubes" metaphor. Ed Felten, a professor of computer science and public affairs at Princeton University, as well as Declan McCullagh, a writer for CNET, both noted that Stevens' "tubes" are not a far cry from the "pipes" that engineers and scientists talk about all the time in a variety of contexts. Both note that Stevens immediately became an object of public ridicule because he so awkwardly voiced a seemingly unintelligent opinion that went against public opinion. Stevens' comments and the public's reaction highlight the fact that the net neutrality debate is one that boils the blood of politicians, corporations, and citizens alike.[7][8]

Net Neutrality in Congress

There has been a lot of Congressional activity regarding net neutrality in the past ten years. Despite public support as well as limited bipartisan support, the legislation mentioned below - except for the Telecommunications Act of 1996 - all represent failed attempts to codify some or all of the principles of net neutrality as federal law. Any proposed Congressional action on the issue has been pro-net neutrality. Members of Congress who oppose net neutrality have not introduced any legislation to support their views, thus perpetuating the anti-net neutrality position that existing laws and the courts are more than capable of handling any issues that may arise.

Telecommunications Act of 1996

The Telecommunications Act of 1996, signed into law by President Clinton, was the first major piece of legislation to directly include the then-nacent Internet.While very specific about how the telephone market should be treated, the Telecommunications Act ultimately leaves regulation of the Internet to the Federal Communications Commission. More specifically, the Act grants the FCC powers to arbitrate "price cap regulation, regulatory forbearance, measures that promote competition in the local telecommunications market, or other regulating methods that remove barriers to infrastructure investment".[9]

The language used in the Act to describe the FCC's powers is rather vague, thus calling into question whether the FCC actually has the legal power to enforce net neutrality regulation. If the FCC attempted to enact net neutrality protections that an ISP did not like, then that ISP might challenge the FCC's fiat in a court. Under the language of the Telecommunications Act, a judge could rule that FCC has no power to enforce how ISPs treat the Internet traffic flowing through their systems. Such uncertainty implies that the best path towards net neutrality legislation lies with Congress.[9]

Internet Freedom and Nondiscrimination Act of 2006, 2008

In 2006, Representative James Sensenbrenner Jr., a Republican from Wisconsin, introduced the Internet Freedom and Nondiscrimination Act to the House Judiciary Committee. The bill amended the Clayton Antitrust Act to prohibit a broadband Internet provider from "failing to provide its services on reasonable and nondiscriminatory terms"[10] and effectively making net neutrality federal law. Internet service providers would have been barred from preventing impairing a person's ablity to access or even offer content over the Internet. Most importantly, if an ISP prioritized any type of Internet traffic, then it must prioritize all data of that class without charging any extra fees.

This law would have provided proponents of net neutrality with almost every protection they might have wanted. ISPs would not be able to unfairly penalize consumers or content providers. But the bill never made its way out of the Judiciary committee. In 2008, Representative John Conyers Jr., a Democrat from Michigan, introduced a nearly identical bill with the same name which also did not become law.[11]

Internet Freedom and Preservation Act of 2009

Introducted by Representative Edward Markey, a Democrat from the Massachusetts 5th District, the Internet Freedom and Preservation Act of 2009 aimed to establish a nation-wide standard of non-discrimination of traffic and openness regarding public Internet access. The bill also required that Internet service providers offer stand-alone Internet access packages to consumers as well as transparency about the speed, nature, limitations, and network management practices of the services being offered. Responsibility for implementing and enforcing the terms of the legislation fell to the FCC. This bill did not become law.[6]

Broadband Internet Fairness Act of 2009

Introduced by Representative Eric Massa, a Democrat from New York, the Broadband Internet Fairness Act required any ISP with over two million subcribers that sought to offer a metered, usage-based Internet service plan to submit their proposal for review by the Federal Trade Commission, with consultation from the FCC. The regulatory bodies would "ensure that such plans are fairly based on cost" and any plan deemed to be "unreasonable" would be declared unlawful. This bill did not become law.[6]

Net Neutrality and the Federal Communications Commission

The FCC claims most of its regulatory power over the Internet landscape from both the Communications Act of 1934 and the Telecommunications Act of 1996, however, any of its decisions or orders can be challenged in court and possibly overturned due to the vagaries of the 1934 and 1996 laws. The FCC's policy- and decision- making has been staunchly in favor of net neutrality, however, as of this writing, recent statements by the current chairman of the FCC indicate that the body may be shifting its position.

Internet Policy Statement of 2005

In the summer of 2005, a Supreme Court decision upheld an FCC policy from 2002 that classified cable Internet services as "interstate information services" under Title I of the Communications Act of 1934, thus entitling the FCC to regulatory authority over such services. The FCC subsequently issued a statement outlining four principles that would guide the body's policymaking:

  1. "consumers are entitled to access the lawful Internet content of their choice"
  2. "consumers are entitled to run applications and services of their choice (subject to the needs of law enforcement)"
  3. "consumers are entitled to connect their choice of legal devices that do not harm the network"
  4. "consumers are entitled to competition among network providers, application and service providers, and content providers"[6]

Comcast and the FCC - 2008

In August 2008, the FCC was made aware that Comcast was actively singling out and blocking peer-to-peer Internet traffic. The FCC felt that Comcast was interfering with their customers' ability to "run applications and services of their choice"[6], as per the commission's 2005 policy statement. The commission did not impose a monetary penalty but did require Comcast to halt its blocking of peer-to-peer applications by the end of the year.

Rather than blocking traffic related to specific applications, Comcast began to manage its network by throttling the throughput of heavy users located in traffic-congested areas. This practice complied the FCC's policy that network management be application agnostic.

Despite the compliant nature of Comcast's new network management practices, the company filed an appeal arguing that the FCC did not have the legal authority to enforce its policies in the first place. The FCC claimed regulatory authority from Title I of Communications Act of 1934. But the court ruled 3-0 that the FCC had overstepped its authority, thus annuling the commission's 2008 directive to Comcast.

The loss in court did not phase FCC Chairman Julius Genachowski. He stated that the decicison "does not change our broadband policy goals, or the ultimate authority of the FCC to act to achieve those goals".[6]

Open Internet Order of 2010

Chairman Genachowski remained true to his word and in December of 2010 set out to expand upon the FCC's policies from 2005. The Open Internet Order's goal was to establishing net neutrality on the Internet through three rules:

  1. "fixed and mobile broadband Internet service providers are required to publically disclose accurate information regarding network management practices, performance, and commercial terms to consumers and as well as content, application, service, and device providers"
  2. "fixed and mobile broadband Internet service providers are both subject, to varying degrees, to no blocking requirements"
  3. "fixed broadband Internet service providers are subject to a “no unreasonable discrimination rule” that states that they shall not unreasonably discriminate in transmitting lawful network traffic over a consumer’s broadband Internet access service"[6]

The Open Internet Order did not explicitly prohibit paid prioritization of Internet traffic, though it noted that this method would likely be considered "unreasonable discrimination". The order also did not explicitly prohibit metered, usage-based pricing plans. If ISPs were prevented from charging heavy users of their networks more money, then ISPs would ultimately be "[forcing] lighter end users of the network to subsidize heavier end users"[6], a phenomena that FCC stated in the Open Internet Order that it would like to prevent.[12]

It is important to note that the third goal of the Open Internet Order applies only to "fixed broadband Internet service providers," not both "fixed and mobile" like the first two goals. Genachowski, though willing to impose net neutrality regulation on fixed ISPs, seemed unwilling to impose such regulation on mobile providers. This decision drew public criticism of both Genachowski and the Open Internet Order. But the chairman maintained his belief that the mobile Internet market needed to be regulated differently, arguing that the wireless market, compared to its wired companion, is more competitive and too different on a technical level to regulate in the exact same manner.[13]

Recent Developments

In early 2013, Chairman Genachowski, who spearheaded the Open Internet Order of 2010, stepped down from his position. President Obama nominated Tom Wheeler, a "former top lobbyist for the cable and wireless industries"[14]. Wheeler's ties to the telecom industry received mixed reactions from the public: some felt that his past experience would be extremely useful as chairman of the FCC; others felt that he was too close to the corporations that he might soon be regulating. Wheeler was confirmed in late 2013. In an interview with "The Verge", Wheeler stated that "[The FCC has] to make sure that where [competition] exists, it continues to exist" and that "[the FCC] is pro-open networks."[15]

But less than a month after that interview, Wheeler presented conflicted views regarding net neutrality. While answering question at Ohio State University, the new chairman said:

"I am a firm believer in the market. I think we’re also going to see a two-sided market where Netflix might say, ‘well, I’ll pay in order to make sure that you might receive, my subscriber receives, the best possible transmission of this movie.’ I think we want to let those kinds of things evolve. We want to observe what happens from that, and we want to make decisions accordingly, but I go back to the fact that the marketplace is where these decisions ought to be made, and the functionality of a competitive marketplace dictates the degree of regulation."[16]

Wheeler's comment was made mere seconds after he said "we stand for an open Internet,"[16] and his comment seems to imply that the current FCC regulations already permit what he is describing, or at the very least Wheeler will seek to change those regulations. His comments immediately drew criticism from consumer advocacy groups who argued that Wheeler was seemingly advocating the exact opposite of net neutrality and countervaling the past decade of FCC policymaking.

Net Neutrality and the Corporations with the "Tubes"

The controversy between Comcast and the FCC is perhaps the most significant battle to date between an ISP and the regulatory body over net neutrality rules. Comcast's actions represent a direct challenge to the rules established by the FCC as well as the FCC's legal authority. Other industry stakeholders have proposed their own plans seeking compromises on net neutrality.

A group of Internet service providers along with Microsoft, Intel, and Google came together to create the Broadband Internet Technical Advisory Group (BITAG or TAG) in 2010. The BITAG was comprised of engineers from the member corporations. The group's stated goal was to evaluate new technologies and try to predit the consequences for end users. The effort seemed to be the industry's attempt to self-regulate. [17]

Corporations take issue with net neutrality primarily because they want do not want to be hindered by law and regulation in efforts to maintain and operate efficient, reliable, and profitable networks. In a 2005 interview with Edward Whitacre, then the CEO of SBC (which soon acquired AT&T), stated his point of view in no uncertain terms:

Q: "How concerned are you about Internet upstarts like Google (GOOG ), MSN, Vonage, and others?"

A: "How do you think they're going to get to customers? Through a broadband pipe. Cable companies have them. We have them. Now what they would like to do is use my pipes free, but I ain't going to let them do that because we have spent this capital and we have to have a return on it. So there's going to have to be some mechanism for these people who use these pipes to pay for the portion they're using. Why should they be allowed to use my pipes?

The Internet can't be free in that sense, because we and the cable companies have made an investment and for a Google or Yahoo! or Vonage or anybody to expect to use these pipes [for] free is nuts!"[18]

Verizon/Google Proposal - 2010

In August 2010, Google and Verizon released a joint proposal for a framework that could be used to guide future net neutrality legislation. Their proposed framework parroted policies from the FCC's Open Internet Order. The proposal included clauses about increasing transparency regarding ISPs' policies, ensuring non-discrimination of content, and appropriate network management. The network management paragraph provided for "[r]easonable network management includes any technically sound practice".[19] The subject of wireless broadband was also briefly touched on but only insofar as to apply increased transparency of how it is managed.

The proposal granted limited regulatory authority to the FCC. It provided for case-by-case enforcement of consumer protection and non-discrimination rules, but specifically stated that the FCC "would have no rulemaking authority with respect to those provisions". The other major concession to the FCC was that the body "would have exclusive authority to oversee broadband Internet access service, but would not have any authority over Internet software applications, content or services".[19]

Critics of the proposal pointed out that, though it seems to superficially support net neutrality, the proposal makes exemptions for "unlawful" content, for wireless services, and for "additional online services" - all vaguely defined in the proposal. Critics argued that these three major exceptions could ultimately be the loopholes that ISPs use to completely circumvent net neutrality.[20]

AT&T, Verizon, Spring, and the SEC - 2012

In early 2012, the Securities and Exchange Commission publicly voiced its support for net neutrality, stating that the principle had become "a significant policy consideration".[21] Previously, the SEC had deemed that net neutrality proposals would affect the day-to-day operations of a company and thus were exempt from shareholder voting. But the SEC's 2012 statement declared that AT&T, Verizon, and Sprint must now include proposals to "publicly commit to operate its wireless broadband network consistent with network neutrality principle"[21] in their next rounds of shareholder voting.

All three companies recommended "no" votes to their shareholders. Over 94% of AT&T's shareholders and 96% of Verizon's shareholders rejected the proposal to implement net neutrality rules within the companies' mobile networks. These decisions obviously disappointed the net neutrality advocates who had lobbied the SEC into forcing the issue onto the ballot. But the results of the vote are consistent with the FCC's Open Internet Order of 2010. [22] [23]

ISPs' Ban on Servers

Much to the chagrin of net neutrality advocates, it has long been standard practice for Internet service providers to ban consumers from operating their own servers of any kind through their connections. ISPs claim that this policy is not actually a violation of the net neutrality rules established by the Open Internet Order. They argure that banning servers is a "...a legitimate [network] management practice" [24] allowed by the Order. Some ISPs even classify peer-to-peer applications as a form of server and claim that they can legally ban such usage as well. Most ISPs do offer business class services that allow servers to be operated.

Though the Open Internet Order's rules state that "fixed broadband providers may not block lawful content, applications, services, or non-harmful devices"[25] it is easy to see the argument that ISPs may make against servers: any sufficiently high-traffic server could be considered harmful to the overall quality of the network.

Critics of such policies feel that ISPs' definitions of a "server" are purposefully vague. The ban on servers is seen to represent "a power grab by ISPs that damages user freedom and chills innovation of different types of Internet-based technologies that don't follow the traditional centralized model".[26]

In the summer of 2013, Google formally issued the terms of service for its new Google Fiber broadband service, which included a complete ban on any and all servers being operated on the Fiber network. Facing a torrent of criticism, Google stated that its policy was in line with established industry standards. Google stood by its decision until the fall of 2013, when it finally amended its Acceptable Use Policy to included "personal, non-commercial use of servers...including using virtual private networks (VPN) to access services in your home and using hardware or applications that include server capabilities for uses like multi-player gaming, video-conferencing, and home security".[27][28][26]

Google's policy change is seen as a victory for net neutrality, however, there seems to be little hope that any other ISPs, especially companies like Comcast, Verizon, or AT&T, will change their server policies in the foreseeable future.

Internet As A Public Utility

In early 2013, a German court ruled that Internet subscribes have the right to compensation from their ISPs if their Internet access is disrupted. The court considered the Internet to be an "essential" part of the lives of Germans; even going as far as to say that the "loss of use of the Internet is comparable to the loss of use of a car".[29]

The status of the Internet in the daily lives of connected Americans is not so clear-cut. There is no law or court document saying that Internet access is a fundamental right for Americans. Though some have begun to call for the Internet be treated and regulated as a public utility. Susan Crawford, a professor at Cardozo Law School who focuses on technology policy, wrote in her 2013 book:

"Truly high-speed wired Internet access is as basic to innovation, economic growth, social communication, and the country’s competitiveness as electricity was a century ago, but a limited number of Americans have access to it, many can’t afford it, and the country has handed control of it over to Comcast and a few other companies."[30]

Crawford believes that it is the federal government's duty to make sure that each citizen has access to low-cost Internet connectivity in the same way that everyone has access to water nd electricity. She argues:

"We treated the telephone industry like a utility and people don’t seem to be surprised by that. High-speed Internet access plays the same role in American life. It’s just that these guys have succeeded in making us think that it’s a luxury."[30]

But the regulation and government involvement that Crawford is calling for seems to be a long way from reality. Though issues of lowering prices and increasing access are not necessarily connected to the net neutrality debate, the points Crawford raises are still ultimately tied to the same central conflict as net neutrality: the relationship between corporations, the marketplace, and the federal government. The Internet-as-public-good and net neutrality debates both emphasize the lack of regulation for a medium as important and swiftly growing as the Internet.

Global Interconnections and Over-The-Top Services

A fundamental and often overlooked component of the Internet is its very backbone infrastructure and the interconnections between large regional networks. In order to create a truly global Internet, ISPs all over the world must agree to interconnect their networks. As the Internet continues to grow and traffic steadily increases across these interconnections, companies have no choice but to upgrade their hardware. When an upgrade is deemed necessary at a certain interconnection, the companies involved negotiate who should be financially responsible for the upgrade: if more traffic is flowing from company A to company B than from B to A, then company B will most likely insist that company A finance the hardware upgrades. If company A refuses and the upgrade is delayed, then traffic will continue to pile up at an already congested interconnection and degrade the overall quality service for users all over the world.[31]

The Open Internet Order of 2010 makes a single reference to such interconnections: "We do not intend our rules to affect existing arrangements for network interconnection, including existing paid peering arrangements."[25] The FCC seems to be purposefully removing itself from a critical component of Internet regulation. When the quality of interconnections degrades and the parties involved cannot come to an agreement, only the consumer suffers.

For large companies like Google or Netflix who do not own and operate backbone infrastructure but still produce an enormous amount of traffic, the only way they can provide their services to the greatest number of users at the highest possible quality is to strike deals with the companies that control interconnections. For example, Netflix can offer to connect caching hardware to the backbone providers and ISPs. But if an ISP refuses to connect a Netflix caching server (or Netflix refuses to pay the fee that the ISP demands), then Netflix must host its own caching server and customers of that ISP will get worse Netflix performance. When Netflix and an ISP do reach an agreement, Netflix becomes an "over-the-top" service (OTT). Traffic to and from Netflix essentially sits on top of other traffic and travels much quicker because packets flowing between the Netflix cache and a customer of that ISP do not have to make as many hops across the network as they normally might. [31][32]

The existence of OTT Internet services creates another interesting challenge for net neutrality. An OTT's services will certainly outperform similar non-OTT services; and this is entirely because the OTT service reached an agreement with an ISP. This is not necessarily due to the direct preferential treatment of individual packets but rather a consequence of geographic proximity, in most cases. Whether or not this is a violation of net neutrality is still an unanswered question.

Technology and Principles

Deep Packet Inspection and the End-To-End Principle

The core technology that enables Internet services providers to discriminate and prioritize individual packets travelling through their networks is deep packet inspection (DPI). In the past few years, networking hardware manufactures have started making firewalls that implement DPI. These firewalls analyze the headers and payload of every packet passing through. They can discriminate between packets from different applications and services, accepting, rejecting, or prioritizing packets based on a ruleset provided by the network administrator. DPI considers information from the network, transport, session, presentation, and application OSI layers. DPI's analysis of a packet's payload is a major improvement over "shallow inspection," which only looked at a packet's headers and made decisions based on the obvious details like source or destination IP addresses and port numbers.[33]

DPI has a wide variety of uses in any network. It can defend against distributed denial of service attacks, detect viruses being transferred to a computer behind a firewall, or even effortlessly separate specific traffic for analysis by law enforcement. By analyzing traffic at the application layer, a network administrator using DPI hardware could detect packets specific to Gmail and even reconstruct emails belonging to that account.

In the context of net neutrality, DPI could be used by ISPs to categorize the different types of data that they might wish to prioritize over other traffic. If an ISP prioritizes its users' streaming video over VoIP, then DPI allows ISPs to distinguish between the two kinds of packets and apply the ISP's ruleset. These capabilities are exactly what advocates of net neutrality fear when they describe "unreasonable discrimination". Currently, such discrimination is rare because few ISPs have adopted widespread DPI and the legal framework for such policies is still in question.[34]

The end-to-end principle is implicit in the overall design of the Internet. While application-level details were left to the clients and servers scattered all around the world, the Internet's lower level infrastructure was implemented in accordance with the end-to-end principle: to "provide only resources of broad utility across applications, while providing to applications useable means for effective sharing of resources and resolution of resource conflicts (network transparency)".[35]

DPI itself does not violate the end-to-end principle. But the widespread introduction of deep packet inspection into the Internet's infrastructure would make resource sharing and conflict resolution at a lower level much more difficult. Suddenly, a network originally designed to only shuttle packets back and forth would be involved in fundamental decisionmaking. In the worst case scenario, all of the nodes implementing DPI would each produce different rulesets for their filters. Every node might share resources and resolve conflict differently from any other node. This dramatically complicates how to model the flow of data across the Internet writ large and might hinder future development and growth.

Net Neutrality and TCP/IP

An important feature of the IP protocol is that two sequentially packets, from the same source and headed to the same destination, may not take the same exact path through the Internet to complete their journeys. Whenever an IP node in the bowels of the Internet receives a packet, it makes a decision about where to send that packet. Sophisticated routing algorithms can measure traffic and send packets to low traffic nodes. But a simplistic implementation will rotate through a list of possible next steps, sending one packet to each location. If some node breaks down, others can simply send traffic elsewhere and the TCP/IP protocol suite guarantees that it will eventually get to the appropriate destination. The IP protocol does not play favorites: it was built to deal with indiscriminate hops from one node to another.[36]

In a world without net neutrality, then the TCP/IP model of the Internet would not be as effective as it is today. For example, say the ISP A sometimes sends packets to nodes controlled by ISP B. But ISP B, thanks to a lack of net neutrality regulation, has decided to downgrade the speed of - or even block - packets coming to it from ISP A. This would mean that ISP A's customers would experience a drop in their quality of service. In order to compensate, ISP A might have to force its routers to send more traffic to the more neighborly ISP C. Now ISP C is getting more traffic than ever before from ISP A, which is beginning to affect the quality of service of its own customers. Naturally, ISP C will be forced to treat traffic from ISP A unfavorably.

This vicious cycle forces ISPs to funnel all of their traffic through a limited pipeline rather than the larger Internet, thus entirely defeating the purpose of the TCP/IP implementation.

Conclusion

The future of net neutrality in the United States is impossible to predict. In the past ten years, so many different efforts to impose net neutrality on the Internet have tried and failed. The idea has seen support in Congress, from the FCC, from the public and within industry, but it simultaneously faces intense opposition from within those very groups.

A primary challenge facing net neutrality is adapting to the ever-changing landscape of the Internet. In 2009, Comcast sought to buyout General Electric's majority stake in NBC. When the deal was announced, speculation abounded about whether the federal government would perform an antitrust investigation into the new pairing of one of the country's largest content owners with one of the country's largest distribution networks. Observers decried the possibility of a merger, arguing that it was a threat to net neutrality and the World Wide Web as a whole. Senator Al Franken used the opportunity to criticize the FCC's Open Internet Order for not being strong enough. On Comcast-NBC specifically, he said "I believe we are facing and will continue to face a growing threat of corporate control on the flow of information in our country".[37][38]

Without rigorously enforced net neutrality, it seems easy to imagine nightmare scenarios resulting from the union of Comcast and NBC. In order to protect and promote its investment, Comcast might prioritize packets containing NBC content from its own xFinity service over those of, say, YouTube or Netflix; or might even block those websites entirely, putting them behind an extra paywall.

In January 2011, the FCC, voting 4 to 1, approved the Comcast-NBC merger, with the lone dissenter saying "At the end of the day, the public interest requires more -- much more -- than it is receiving".[39]

But the Comcast-NBC deal, as well as the recent appointment of a new Chairman of the FCC, highlight a fundamental issue with the telecommunications industry. The regulatory body responsible for enforcing any net neutrality rules is plagued by a "revolving door": key decisions have strong ties to the telecom industry. In May 2011, one of the FCC commissiorers who had voted for the merger, Meredith Attwell Baker, had announced her plans to leave the FCC for an executive position within Comcast.[40] The new Chairman of the FCC, Tom Wheeler, is a former telecommunications lobbyist.

If any progress is to be made in ensuring net neutrality's place in the Internet, then a middle-ground must be found between industry and government, profit margins and fairness. There are systemic issues that must be solved, from removing the clout of industry from the FCC to regulating Internet interconnection points.
